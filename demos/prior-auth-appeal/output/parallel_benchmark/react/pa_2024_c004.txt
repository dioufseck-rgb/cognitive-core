**1. Appeal Request and Denial Reason:**
The member requested prior authorization for an ACDF at C5-C6 for severe cervical stenosis with cord contact, indicating a prophylactic intervention due to high risk of myelopathy from minor trauma, exacerbated by his occupation as a construction worker.

The initial denial (Dr. Patricia Huang, MD) cited HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024, reason code MED-003, stating: "Cervical stenosis documented but no neurological deficit on examination. Member has axial neck pain only. No radiculopathy, no myelopathy documented. Surgical intervention not indicated for axial pain without neurological deficit under CC-SPINE-2024."

**2. Clinical Record Summary:**
Mr. Raymond Flores is a 54-year-old construction worker with severe cervical stenosis at C5-C6. An MRI (2024-09-15) revealed a central canal diameter of 6mm (normal >13mm) with cord contact, but no T2 signal change or myelomalacia. His neurological examination (2024-09-20) was completely normal, with no signs of radiculopathy or myelopathy, and he reported only axial neck pain (5/10).

Conservative treatment included:
*   8 weeks (24 sessions) of physical therapy, resulting in modest pain improvement (7/10 to 5/10). The PT note explicitly stated "No neurological symptoms to treat."
*   3 months of Naproxen (moderate pain relief) and 6 weeks of Cyclobenzaprine (sleep improvement).
*   One C5-C6 epidural steroid injection (60% pain relief lasting 6 weeks).

The requesting surgeon, Dr. Karen Davis, noted: "This patient has critically narrow canal diameter. Any significant cervical trauma would likely cause acute spinal cord injury. Given his occupation operating heavy equipment, the risk of such trauma is elevated. Prophylactic decompression is indicated to prevent catastrophic injury."

**3. Hierarchy of Authority:**
The review adheres to the following hierarchy: State Law > Clinical Standard > Plan Criteria.

---

**4. Analysis:**

**A. Application of Plan Criteria (HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024):**
The plan's criteria (Section 2.1) require a confirmed diagnosis of cervical radiculopathy (2.1a), cervical myelopathy (2.1b), or cervical disc herniation with objective neurological deficit (2.1c). Mr. Flores's clinical presentation, specifically the normal neurological exam and absence of symptoms, does not meet these criteria as written, which define myelopathy and radiculopathy based on *symptomatic presentation*. Section 3.1 excludes "Surgery requested solely for axial neck pain without documented radiculopathy or myelopathy."

The plan's denial is consistent with a literal application of these criteria, concluding that without symptoms of myelopathy or radiculopathy, and with only axial neck pain, the surgery is not indicated. The plan's criteria do not explicitly address "prophylactic" surgery for severe asymptomatic stenosis with cord contact in high-risk individuals.

Regarding conservative treatment (Section 2.2), Mr. Flores met the minimum requirements for PT (8 weeks), pharmacotherapy (two agents for 4+ weeks each), and interventional treatment (one epidural). However, the plan's denial was not based on unmet conservative treatment requirements, but rather on the lack of a qualifying diagnosis.

**B. Application of California Regulatory Requirements (State Law):**

1.  **CHSC § 1374.32(b) — Standard of Review for IMR:** This section mandates that a service meeting "generally accepted standards of medical practice" SHALL be approved, regardless of whether the plan's internal criteria are more restrictive. This provision is critical if clinical standards support the requested prophylactic surgery.
2.  **CIC § 10169.5(a)(3) — Physical Therapy as Prerequisite:** This state law prohibits conditioning surgical approval on PT where "The condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address."
    *   **Application:** Mr. Flores's MRI shows severe central canal stenosis (6mm diameter) with cord contact. This constitutes objective evidence of structural neurological compromise. Physical therapy cannot reduce the structural narrowing or the risk of acute spinal cord injury from trauma. While the patient completed PT for his axial pain, this law supports waiving PT requirements as a prerequisite for addressing the *underlying structural risk* that the prophylactic surgery aims to mitigate.
3.  **DMHC All-Plan Letter APL 22-014 — Myelopathy and Urgent Conditions:** This APL emphasizes that plans "SHALL NOT require completion of a full course of conservative treatment as a prerequisite to surgical authorization" for conditions where delay of treatment risks irreversible harm, provided certain conditions are met. While the MRI did not show myelomalacia (cord signal change), the APL explicitly identifies *cord compression* as a finding that can indicate a risk of irreversible harm (II.1), and speaks more broadly about "conditions where delay of treatment risks irreversible harm" (II.2).
    *   **Application:** The surgeon's strong documentation highlights the "critically narrow canal diameter" and the high likelihood of "acute spinal cord injury" from "any significant cervical trauma" given the member's "occupation operating heavy equipment." This clearly points to a "risk of irreversible harm" that the prophylactic surgery is intended to prevent, aligning with the spirit of the APL.

**C. Application of Clinical Standards (AANS/CNS 2023, NASS 2020, AO Spine 2021):**

1.  **Myelopathy vs. Radiculopathy:** Clinical guidelines emphasize the distinct treatment algorithms for myelopathy and radiculopathy, noting that conservative treatment evidence is primarily for radiculopathy. Mr. Flores does not have symptomatic myelopathy or radiculopathy.
2.  **Physical Therapy for Myelopathy:** Clinical guidelines (AO Spine 2021) state, "There is no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy. Delaying surgery in such patients to complete a physical therapy trial is not supported by the evidence and may result in neurological deterioration." While Mr. Flores does not have symptomatic myelopathy, the underlying *structural cause* (severe stenosis with cord contact) which leads to myelopathy is present. PT for pain relief does not address this structural risk.
3.  **Surgical Indications (AANS/CNS Tier 1):** The "TIER 1 — STRONG INDICATION (surgery recommended)" includes "Acute severe myelopathy with risk of permanent deficit" (d). While Mr. Flores does not have *acute myelopathy*, the surgeon is arguing that he has an imminent "risk of permanent deficit" from acute spinal cord injury due to trauma, which the surgery aims to prevent. The clinical standard prioritizes prevention of permanent neurological deficit.
4.  **Prophylactic Surgery:** While major guidelines primarily discuss symptomatic conditions, the concept of prophylactic decompression for severe, asymptomatic spinal cord compression in high-risk individuals (e.g., athletes, specific occupations with high trauma risk) to prevent catastrophic traumatic myelopathy is a recognized aspect of prudent spine surgery practice, aligning with the general goal of preventing irreversible harm. The "critically narrow canal diameter" with "cord contact" and the "high trauma risk" due to his occupation elevate this case beyond simple "axial neck pain."

**D. Conflict Resolution:**
The plan's denial is based on its criteria that require *symptomatic* neurological deficits or myelopathy and exclude surgery for "axial neck pain only." This interpretation fails to account for the unique clinical scenario of severe asymptomatic spinal cord compromise with high-risk occupation, where prophylactic surgery is requested to prevent a catastrophic neurological injury.

California state law, particularly **CHSC § 1374.32(b)**, mandates approval if the service meets "generally accepted standards of medical practice" even if the plan's criteria are more restrictive. The surgeon's rationale for preventing acute spinal cord injury in a patient with severe stenosis and cord contact, particularly given the elevated trauma risk of his occupation, aligns with the prudent clinical judgment encompassed by generally accepted standards for preventing neurological catastrophe. Furthermore, **CIC § 10169.5(a)(3)** directly overrides any conservative treatment prerequisite when objective structural neurological compromise is present that PT cannot address.

The plan's denial statement mischaracterizes the primary reason for the surgery as treatment solely for "axial neck pain" and does not adequately address the documented severe structural risk and the prophylactic nature of the requested intervention.

---

**5. Disposition:**
**OVERTURN**

**6. Warrant:**
The denial for CPT code 22551 (ACDF C5-C6) for Raymond Flores is hereby OVERTURNED. The plan's denial, based on the absence of current neurological deficits or myelopathy and classifying the request as surgery solely for axial neck pain, is inconsistent with California state law and generally accepted standards of medical practice given the specific clinical circumstances.

1.  **California Health & Safety Code § 1374.32(b) Override:** The requesting physician's rationale for prophylactic decompression to prevent acute spinal cord injury in a patient with objectively documented severe cervical stenosis (6mm canal diameter) with cord contact, and a high-risk occupation (construction worker operating heavy equipment), constitutes a generally accepted standard of medical practice for preventing catastrophic neurological injury. While the member is currently asymptomatic, the clinical guidelines (AANS/CNS, NASS, AO Spine) implicitly support interventions to prevent irreversible harm and emphasize the poor outcomes of delayed surgery once myelopathy becomes symptomatic, especially with structural cord compromise. Therefore, under state law, the service must be approved regardless of the plan's more restrictive criteria.

2.  **California Insurance Code § 10169.5(a)(3) Applies:** The MRI findings of severe central canal stenosis with cord contact represent "objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address." Physical therapy, though completed for axial pain, cannot reduce the critical canal narrowing or prevent the risk of traumatic spinal cord injury. Therefore, any plan criteria requiring further conservative treatment for the underlying structural risk are overridden by state law.

3.  **Mischaracterization of Medical Necessity:** The plan's denial mischaracterizes the request as being "solely for axial neck pain." The primary medical necessity articulated by the surgeon is for prophylactic intervention to prevent catastrophic acute spinal cord injury due to the severe anatomical risk and the member's high-risk occupation. The plan's criteria failed to adequately address this specific, medically necessary prophylactic indication.

Based on the hierarchy of authority, California state law and generally accepted clinical principles for preventing neurological injury supersede the plan's internal criteria in this case.
