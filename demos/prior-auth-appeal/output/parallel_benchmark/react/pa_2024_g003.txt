### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This appeal involves a prior authorization denial for an Anterior Cervical Discectomy and Fusion (ACDF) for mild cervical myelopathy. The denial was based on the member's failure to complete the plan's standard conservative treatment requirements, specifically regarding the duration of physical therapy, number of pharmacotherapy agents, and absence of an interventional procedure.

Upon review of the clinical record, relevant state laws (California Insurance Code § 10169.5, Health & Safety Code § 1374.32, DMHC APL 22-014), and generally accepted clinical standards (AANS/CNS, NASS, AO Spine), it is determined that the plan's denial should be **OVERTURNED**.

While the member did not strictly meet all of HealthFirst's conservative treatment criteria, the application of these criteria to a diagnosis of cervical myelopathy is inconsistent with California state law and generally accepted clinical standards of care. Specifically, state law overrides the physical therapy requirement for conditions involving structural neurological compromise, and clinical guidelines indicate that mandatory conservative treatment durations, particularly for physical therapy, are not evidence-based for myelopathy.

---

### CASE BACKGROUND

Bernard Achebe, a 61-year-old male, was diagnosed with mild cervical myelopathy at C5-C6. MRI imaging dated 2024-10-01 showed moderate central cord compression at C5-C6, with a canal diameter of 8mm and cord contact, but no T2 signal change or myelomalacia. Neurological exam dated 2024-10-05 confirmed myelopathy with bilateral Hoffman signs and mild hyperreflexia. Functional status was noted as fully independent with an mJOA score of 15/18 (mild myelopathy), and symptoms stable for 6 months.

The treating neurologist, Dr. Osei, declared a need for "immediate surgical intervention," stating that "further delay is medically contraindicated" and risks "irreversible neurological deterioration." Conservative treatment included 3 weeks of physical therapy (halted by Dr. Osei due to myelopathy concern) and 6 weeks of Baclofen (one pharmacotherapy agent). No interventional procedures were performed.

HealthFirst denied the prior authorization request, citing non-completion of 6 weeks of physical therapy, use of only one pharmacotherapy agent, and absence of an interventional procedure as per their standard pathway.

### REVIEW AND ANALYSIS

The appeal determination will follow the hierarchy: **State law > Clinical standard > Plan criteria.**

**1. Primary Clinical Presentation:**
The member's presenting diagnosis is "Mild cervical myelopathy C5-C6," supported by MRI findings of central cord compression and neurological exam findings of bilateral Hoffman signs and hyperreflexia (upper motor neuron signs). This confirms a diagnosis of myelopathy, which is distinct from radiculopathy and has different treatment algorithms (Source: Clinical Reference Summary, Section 1.1).

**2. Application of Plan Criteria (HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024):**
*   **2.1 Diagnosis:** Met (Cervical myelopathy).
*   **2.2 Conservative Treatment Requirement (Standard Pathway):** Not met.
    *   (a) Physical therapy: 3 weeks completed; 6 required.
    *   (b) Pharmacotherapy: One agent (Baclofen) completed; two agents required.
    *   (c) Interventional treatment: None completed; one required.
*   **2.2 Exception (Myelopathy Pathway):** Not fully met.
    *   (a) Physical therapy: 3 weeks completed; 4 weeks minimum required for myelopathy pathway.
    *   (b) Pharmacotherapy: One agent completed; full requirement (two agents) remains.
    *   (c) Interventional treatment: Waived if physician documents risk. Dr. Osei halted PT, citing "myelopathy concern," but did not explicitly document that interventional treatment posed a risk of neurological deterioration.
*   **2.3 Imaging Requirement:** Met (MRI confirms C5-C6 pathology).
*   **2.4 Physician Documentation:** Partially met. Dr. Osei provided rationale and urgency declaration, but the conservative treatment was not "completed" per plan definition.

Based solely on HealthFirst's plan criteria, the denial appears justified, as neither the standard nor the myelopathy exception's full requirements were satisfied. However, state law and clinical standards must be applied.

**3. Application of California Regulatory Requirements (State Law):**

*   **CIC § 10169.5 (Physical Therapy as Prerequisite):** This statute prohibits a health insurer from conditioning approval of surgery on PT completion when:
    *   **(a)(1) Physician documents PT is contraindicated or unlikely to benefit:** Dr. Osei halted PT, citing "myelopathy concern," and declared that "further delay is medically contraindicated." While the member's myelopathy is described as "mild" and "stable" in the clinical record, the treating neurologist's professional judgment that PT is not beneficial for the underlying myelopathic condition, leading him to halt it, aligns with this exception. Physical therapy is generally not expected to reverse spinal cord compression or upper motor neuron signs associated with myelopathy.
    *   **(a)(3) The condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address:** The member has objective evidence of "moderate central cord compression" at C5-C6 on MRI, coupled with "bilateral Hoffman signs" and "hyperreflexia" on neurological exam. Cervical myelopathy by definition is a structural neurological compromise of the spinal cord itself, which physical therapy cannot surgically decompress or directly reverse. Therefore, the plan's requirement for a minimum duration of physical therapy is overridden by CIC § 10169.5(a)(3).
    *   **Conclusion:** The plan's physical therapy requirement is specifically prohibited by state law under these circumstances.

*   **DMHC All-Plan Letter APL 22-014 (Myelopathy and Urgent Conditions):** This APL states plans SHALL NOT require full conservative treatment when objective imaging shows *progressive pathology* (cord signal change, myelomalacia, or progressive herniation) AND physician documents conservative failure AND surgery is indicated.
    *   The member's MRI explicitly states "No T2 signal change. No myelomalacia." The clinical record indicates "symptom duration: 6 months, stable," and "mJOA score 15/18 (mild myelopathy)." Thus, there is no objective evidence of *progressive pathology* or *myelomalacia* on record, which are key conditions for this specific exception in APL 22-014.
    *   While Dr. Osei declared the condition to be "severe and progressive" and of "urgency," the objective clinical findings (stable symptoms, mild mJOA, no myelomalacia) do not fully corroborate this specific characterization.
    *   **Conclusion:** The specific waiver conditions outlined in DMHC APL 22-014 for progressive pathology/myelomalacia are not met in this case. However, the APL also states (II.3) that "Plans shall not apply internal criteria that are more restrictive than generally accepted standards of medical practice for conditions identified in this guidance." This general principle is relevant in conjunction with clinical standards.

*   **CHSC § 1374.32(b) (Standard of Review for IMR):** This section is critical, stating that the IMR standard (generally accepted standards of medical practice) does NOT require consistency with the plan's internal coverage criteria. If the service meets generally accepted standards, it SHALL be approved regardless of more restrictive plan criteria.

**4. Application of Clinical Standard (AANS/CNS 2023, NASS 2020, AO Spine 2021):**

*   **Distinction between Myelopathy and Radiculopathy:** Clinical guidelines emphasize that myelopathy and radiculopathy are different diseases with different treatment algorithms. Applying conservative treatment protocols derived from radiculopathy evidence to myelopathy is considered a "category error" (Source: Clinical Reference Summary, Section 1.1).
*   **Physical Therapy for Myelopathy:**
    *   The AO Spine Guidelines (2021) state: "There is no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy. Delaying surgery in such patients to complete a physical therapy trial is not supported by the evidence and may result in neurological deterioration." While the patient has "mild" myelopathy, this guidance strongly suggests that fixed PT duration requirements for myelopathy are not evidence-based.
    *   NASS Guidelines (2020) state that conservative treatment *may be considered for mild cervical myelopathy as initial management, with close monitoring for progression.* However, no specific mandatory duration is established.
    *   **Conclusion:** The plan's rigid 6-week (or even 4-week) physical therapy requirement for myelopathy is not supported by high-quality evidence from generally accepted clinical guidelines.
*   **Surgical Indications for Myelopathy (AANS/CNS 2023):**
    *   The patient's condition of mild myelopathy (mJOA 15/18) with objective cord compression and UMN signs (Hoffman, hyperreflexia) would fall under TIER 2 ("Reasonable Indication") for surgery, which applies to "Mild myelopathy with stable symptoms after adequate conservative trial."
    *   The controversy lies in what constitutes an "adequate conservative trial" when state law and clinical evidence challenge the *plan's* definition of adequacy for myelopathy. Given the lack of evidence for mandatory conservative treatment durations in myelopathy and the presence of objective cord compression with UMN signs, surgery is a reasonable option under generally accepted standards, especially after 3 weeks of PT and a course of pharmacotherapy that did not resolve the neurological signs.
*   **Pharmacotherapy and Interventional Procedures for Myelopathy:** Similar to PT, clinical guidelines do not establish mandatory, specific pharmacotherapy agents or interventional procedures as prerequisites for surgery in myelopathy cases with objective cord compression and UMN signs. While these treatments may be considered, mandating a specific number or type before surgery, particularly when the treating physician indicates they are not resolving the underlying condition, may be more restrictive than generally accepted standards.

**5. Conflict Resolution (State Law > Clinical Standard > Plan Criteria):**

*   The plan's denial is primarily based on the conservative treatment requirements.
*   **State law (CIC § 10169.5)** explicitly overrides the plan's requirement for physical therapy, as the member's condition involves structural neurological compromise that PT cannot address.
*   **Clinical standards** (AO Spine, NASS) demonstrate that mandatory physical therapy durations for myelopathy are not evidence-based and constitute a category error. They also do not mandate specific numbers of pharmacotherapy agents or interventional procedures as prerequisites for surgery in myelopathy.
*   Therefore, the plan's criteria are more restrictive than both California state law and generally accepted standards of medical practice. CHSC § 1374.32(b) dictates that service meeting generally accepted standards shall be approved regardless of the plan's more restrictive internal criteria.

While the "mild" and "stable" nature of the myelopathy, and the absence of myelomalacia, temper the treating physician's strong declaration of "immediate urgency," they do not negate the fundamental principle that the plan's conservative treatment requirements for myelopathy are not justified under state law and current clinical evidence. The presence of objective cord compression and upper motor neuron signs confirms the myelopathic condition, for which specific conservative treatment requirements are not globally evidenced.

---

### DISPOSITION

**OVERTURNED**

### WARRANT

The prior authorization denial for CPT 22551 (ACDF C5-C6) for Bernard Achebe is **OVERTURNED**.

The denial was based on the member's failure to complete conservative treatment requirements as per HealthFirst's policy, specifically related to physical therapy duration, number of pharmacotherapy agents, and interventional procedures. However, this determination is superseded by higher authority sources:

1.  **California State Law Overrides Physical Therapy Requirement:** California Insurance Code § 10169.5(a)(3) prohibits a health insurer from conditioning approval of a surgical procedure on completion of physical therapy where "The condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address." The member's diagnosis of cervical myelopathy with "moderate central cord compression" on MRI and "bilateral Hoffman signs" and "hyperreflexia" on neurological exam constitutes clear objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot resolve. Furthermore, the treating neurologist's decision to halt physical therapy due to "myelopathy concern" aligns with CIC § 10169.5(a)(1), which waives the PT requirement if the physician documents it is unlikely to benefit. Therefore, the plan's denial based on insufficient physical therapy is not permissible under state law.
    *(Source: California Insurance Code § 10169.5(a)(1) & (a)(3))*

2.  **Plan Criteria Are More Restrictive Than Clinical Standards for Myelopathy:** Generally accepted clinical standards for cervical myelopathy do not support mandatory fixed durations of conservative treatment, particularly physical therapy, as a prerequisite for surgery.
    *   The AO Spine Guidelines (2021) state there is "no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy." Even for mild myelopathy, while conservative management may be considered, fixed durations are not evidence-based, and applying radiculopathy-derived PT requirements to myelopathy is deemed a "category error."
    *   The plan's requirements for two pharmacotherapy agents and an interventional procedure for myelopathy, when no specific mandatory conservative treatment protocol is universally endorsed by major spine societies for myelopathy with objective cord compression and UMN signs, are more restrictive than generally accepted standards.
    *   California Health and Safety Code § 1374.32(b) mandates that a service meeting generally accepted standards of medical practice shall be approved regardless of whether the plan's internal criteria are more restrictive.
    *(Source: Clinical Reference Summary Sections 1.1, 2.1, 2.2, 3.1; CHSC § 1374.32(b); DMHC APL 22-014 II.3)*

While the member's myelopathy is classified as "mild" and "stable," and lacks myelomalacia (factors that would typically indicate less urgency), the underlying condition is still myelopathy with objective cord compression and upper motor neuron signs. The plan's conservative treatment requirements, as applied, are inconsistent with both state law and current clinical evidence for this diagnosis. The treating neurologist's clinical judgment, while potentially overstating the "immediate" urgency, reasonably concluded that further conservative treatment, particularly physical therapy, was not appropriate for the myelopathy.

For these reasons, the service requested meets generally accepted standards of medical practice and the denial based on unfulfilled conservative treatment requirements, particularly physical therapy, is inconsistent with California law. Therefore, the prior authorization request must be approved.
