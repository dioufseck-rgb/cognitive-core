**PURPOSE OF REVIEW:**

The initial prior authorization request was denied by Dr. Alan Foster, MD, on the basis of **MED-003**. The stated reasons were:
1.  "Myelopathy exception does not apply. MRI T2 signal change is minimal and may represent artifact. Plan's imaging reviewer notes this finding is below threshold for clinical significance."

**SUMMARY OF APPEAL BASIS:**
The treating physician's appeal highlights three key points:
1.  Radiologist (original) specifically noted T2 signal change in report – not artifact.
2.  Second radiologist opinion (treating hospital) confirms early myelomalacia.
3.  Treating physician has documented myelopathy on clinical grounds independently of imaging.

**CLINICAL SUMMARY:**
Helen Brooks, a 49-year-old female, presents with early cervical myelopathy.
*   **Presenting Diagnosis:** Early cervical myelopathy, with myelomalacia status disputed between the plan's radiologist and the treating physician's radiologist.
*   **Imaging:**
    *   Original MRI (2024-10-01): C4-C5 disc herniation with cord contact. "Possible early T2 signal change — equivocal, may represent artifact. Clinical correlation required."
    *   Second MRI Read (2024-10-15 by Radiologist B, treating hospital): C4-C5 cord compression with *early myelomalacia*. "T2 hyperintensity is reproducible on multiple sequences and represents early cord signal change, not artifact. Consistent with early myelopathy."
    *   Plan Radiologist Review (2024-11-05): "T2 signal change is below threshold for clinical significance. Not consistent with myelomalacia. Represents artifact or normal variant."
*   **Neurological Exam (2024-10-05):** Reveals significant Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) signs including bilateral Hoffman signs, bilateral hyperreflexia in the upper extremities, and subtle balance impairment on tandem gait. No lower extremity findings were noted.
*   **Conservative Treatment:**
    *   **Physical Therapy:** 8 weeks (24 sessions) completed. No improvement in UMN signs.
    *   **Pharmacotherapy:** Gabapentin 400mg TID for 3 months (partial benefit) and Cyclobenzaprine for 6 weeks (minimal benefit).
    *   **Interventional Procedures:** Not performed. The treating physician documented that epidural steroid injections were considered relatively contraindicated due to the presence of cord compression and myelopathy concern.

---

**REVIEWER'S ANALYSIS:**

This appeal determination applies the three-source hierarchy: **State Law > Clinical Standard > Plan Criteria.**

**1. Identification of Primary Clinical Presentation:**
The clinical record, particularly the neurological exam findings (bilateral Hoffman signs, hyperreflexia, balance impairment) in conjunction with MRI demonstrating cord compression, unequivocally establishes a diagnosis of **Cervical Myelopathy**. The plan's initial denial implicitly acknowledges this by attempting to apply a "myelopathy exception."

**2. Application of HealthFirst Plan Criteria (CC-SPINE-2024):**

*   **2.1 Diagnosis Requirement:** Member meets criterion 2.1(b) for Cervical Myelopathy ("spinal cord compression with myelopathic symptoms [gait disturbance, bilateral upper extremity weakness, hyperreflexia, positive Hoffman sign]"). The neurological exam findings directly support this. *This criterion is met.*
*   **2.2 Conservative Treatment Requirement:**
    *   **(a) Physical Therapy:** 8 weeks completed, exceeding the 6-week minimum. *Met duration-wise.*
    *   **(b) Pharmacotherapy:** Two agents (Gabapentin, Cyclobenzaprine) used for durations exceeding 4 weeks each. *Met.*
    *   **(c) Interventional Treatment:** This was not completed, which was a core reason for the plan's denial under the "standard pathway."
*   **EXCEPTION 2.2(A) — MYELOPATHY PATHWAY:** This exception reduces the PT requirement to 4 weeks (met by 8 weeks completed) and waives the interventional treatment requirement if the treating physician documents that it "poses risk of neurological deterioration."
    *   **Plan's Denial:** The plan's denial explicitly states, "Myelopathy exception does not apply. MRI T2 signal change is minimal and may represent artifact." This indicates the plan did *not* apply its own myelopathy exception based on its internal radiologist's interpretation of imaging.
    *   **Reviewer's Finding:** The plan's internal radiologist's opinion (minimal T2 signal change/artifact) directly contradicts the second radiologist's definitive finding of "early myelomalacia" and the treating physician's clinical documentation of myelopathy. Crucially, the member presents with clear **clinical signs of myelopathy** (bilateral Hoffman, hyperreflexia, balance impairment) as per the plan's own definition in 2.1(b), which are independent of the myelomalacia dispute. Furthermore, the treating physician *did* document risk for interventional treatment ("epidural steroid injections are relatively contraindicated in cord compression"). Therefore, the plan *should have applied its own myelopathy exception*, which would waive the interventional treatment requirement. The plan's decision not to apply its exception is a misapplication of its own policy.
*   **2.3 Imaging Requirement:** Current imaging demonstrates structural pathology (cord compression). *Met.*
*   **2.4 Physician Documentation:** Not cited as a reason for denial; assumed met.

**3. Application of California Regulatory Requirements (State Law):**

*   **DMHC All-Plan Letter APL 22-014 (Myelopathy and Urgent Conditions):** This state guidance is highly pertinent and directly applicable.
    *   **II.1 Myelopathy Classification:** The APL states that "cervical myelopathy with objective evidence of cord compression and myelomalacia on MRI constitutes a condition with potential for irreversible harm if untreated. Plans SHALL treat such cases as urgent." Here, objective cord compression is present. While myelomalacia is disputed by the plan's reviewer, the treating team's radiologist and physician have confirmed it, and the presence of UMN signs strongly supports the urgency.
    *   **II.2 Conservative Treatment Exceptions:** This section states that plans "SHALL NOT require completion of a full course of conservative treatment" when:
        *   (a) "Objective imaging evidence demonstrates progressive pathology (cord signal change, myelomalacia, or progressive herniation on serial imaging)": The second radiologist's report confirms "early myelomalacia" (cord signal change), satisfying this criterion.
        *   (b) "Treating physician provides documentation that conservative treatment has been attempted and has failed to arrest progression": PT completed 8 weeks with "no improvement in UMN signs," and pharmacotherapy provided only partial/minimal benefit. The physician documented the contraindication to interventional therapy. This criterion is met.
        *   (c) "Surgical intervention is indicated under generally accepted clinical guidelines": As detailed in the Clinical Standards section below, surgery is strongly indicated.
        *   The APL specifically states, "The presence of myelomalacia on MRI is specifically identified as a finding that may support waiver of standard conservative treatment requirements even where a full course has not been completed, when the treating physician documents urgency." This directly applies to Helen Brooks' case.
    *   **II.3 Plan Criteria Vs. Clinical Standard:** The APL mandates that plans "shall not apply internal criteria that are more restrictive than generally accepted standards of medical practice."

*   **CIC § 10169.5 (Physical Therapy as Prerequisite):**
    *   **(a)(3):** This section states that a plan shall not condition approval of surgery on PT completion where "The condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address." Helen Brooks' diagnosis of cervical myelopathy with cord compression, UMN signs, and myelomalacia represents objective structural neurological compromise that PT cannot resolve. While the plan did not deny based solely on PT, this law reinforces the inappropriateness of prolonged conservative measures for this condition.

**4. Application of Clinical Standard (AANS/CNS 2023, NASS 2020, AO Spine 2021):**

*   **Distinction between Myelopathy and Radiculopathy:** Clinical guidelines emphasize that myelopathy and radiculopathy are fundamentally different conditions requiring distinct treatment algorithms. Requirements for conservative treatment are largely derived from radiculopathy studies and are not applicable to myelopathy. Helen Brooks clearly has myelopathy (UMN signs).
*   **Myelomalacia Significance:** The presence of myelomalacia (confirmed by the treating hospital's radiologist) signifies existing cord injury, higher risk of permanent deficit, and reduced likelihood of recovery if surgery is delayed. It is considered a strong indication for surgical intervention and a contraindication to extended conservative treatment trials (AO Spine 2021, AANS/CNS 2023).
*   **Conservative Treatment for Myelopathy:**
    *   AO Spine Guidelines (2021) state, "There is no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy. Delaying surgery in such patients to complete a physical therapy trial is not supported by the evidence and may result in neurological deterioration." Helen Brooks' 8 weeks of PT showed no improvement in UMN signs, indicating a functional plateau for her myelopathy.
    *   No high-quality evidence supports a defined minimum duration of conservative treatment for myelopathy.
*   **Surgical Indications:** Helen Brooks meets multiple TIER 1 strong surgical indications (AANS/CNS Joint Guidelines 2023):
    *   (a) Moderate myelopathy (based on UMN signs) with objective cord compression on MRI.
    *   (c) Myelomalacia on MRI (as interpreted by treating hospital's radiologist).
    *   These guidelines expressly state that surgery is recommended, and the clinical standard does NOT require completion of a minimum course of physical therapy or interventional procedures as a prerequisite for this presentation.

**5. Conflict Resolution and Disposition:**

The plan's denial is based on its internal criteria requiring completion of an interventional procedure under its "standard pathway," and its refusal to apply its "myelopathy exception" due to a disputed interpretation of imaging.

*   **Plan's Criteria vs. Clinical Evidence:** The plan's internal radiologist's interpretation of "minimal" T2 signal change, leading to dismissal of myelomalacia and the myelopathy exception, is contradicted by the second radiologist's read and the overwhelming clinical evidence of myelopathy (UMN signs). The plan failed to correctly apply its *own* myelopathy exception, which would waive the interventional procedure requirement given the documented risk.
*   **Plan's Criteria vs. State Law:** California State Law (DMHC APL 22-014) explicitly prohibits plans from requiring a full course of conservative treatment (including interventional procedures) for myelopathy with objective imaging evidence of progressive pathology (myelomalacia), documented conservative treatment failure, and surgical indication under clinical guidelines. All these conditions are met in Helen Brooks' case. This state law *overrides* HealthFirst's more restrictive criteria for this time-sensitive neurological condition. Additionally, CIC § 10169.5(a)(3) supports waiver of PT requirements for structural neurological compromise.
*   **Plan's Criteria vs. Clinical Standard:** Major spine society guidelines (AANS/CNS, AO Spine) establish that for moderate myelopathy with cord compression and myelomalacia, surgery is strongly indicated, and delaying surgery for extended conservative treatment (such as mandatory interventional procedures) is not supported by evidence and carries a risk of neurological deterioration. The plan's standard pathway requirements are a "category error" when applied to myelopathy, and are more restrictive than generally accepted medical practice.

According to the established hierarchy, **State Law and Clinical Standards take precedence over Plan Criteria.** The plan's denial is inconsistent with California regulations and generally accepted medical standards for the treatment of cervical myelopathy.

---

**DISPOSITION: OVERTURN**

**WARRANT:**
The denial for ACDF C4-C5 for Helen Brooks is hereby **OVERTURNED** for the following reasons:

1.  **Misapplication of Plan's Own Myelopathy Exception:** The member clearly meets the plan's own definition of cervical myelopathy (Section 2.1(b)) based on objective neurological findings (bilateral Hoffman signs, hyperreflexia, balance impairment) and MRI evidence of cord compression. The treating physician documented that interventional treatment posed a risk of neurological deterioration due to cord compression, which should have triggered the waiver of interventional treatment under the plan's Myelopathy Pathway (Section 2.2(A) Exception). The plan's denial of this exception based solely on its internal radiologist's subjective interpretation of T2 signal change as "minimal/artifact," contradicting a second radiologist's finding of myelomalacia, is erroneous and a misapplication of its own policy.
2.  **Violation of California State Law (DMHC APL 22-014):** This All-Plan Letter explicitly prohibits health plans from requiring completion of a full course of conservative treatment (including interventional procedures) as a prerequisite for surgery when:
    *   Objective imaging (second radiologist's read) demonstrates "early myelomalacia" (cord signal change).
    *   The treating physician documented that conservative treatment (PT, pharmacotherapy) failed to arrest progression of UMN signs, and that interventional treatment was contraindicated due to myelopathy risk.
    *   Surgical intervention is indicated under generally accepted clinical guidelines for cervical myelopathy with cord compression and myelomalacia.
    This APL directly mandates approval in such circumstances, overriding the plan's more restrictive conservative treatment requirements.
3.  **Conflict with Generally Accepted Clinical Standards:** Major spine society guidelines (AANS/CNS 2023, AO Spine 2021, NASS 2020) uniformly classify moderate cervical myelopathy with objective cord compression and myelomalacia as a TIER 1 strong surgical indication. These guidelines explicitly state that delaying surgery for mandatory conservative treatment trials (such as prolonged PT or interventional injections) is not supported by evidence and may lead to irreversible neurological deterioration. The plan's "standard pathway" requirements for interventional procedures constitute a "category error" when applied to cervical myelopathy and are more restrictive than generally accepted medical practice.
4.  **Objective Structural Neurological Compromise (CIC § 10169.5(a)(3)):** The member's condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise (cord compression, UMN signs, myelomalacia) that physical therapy or interventional injections cannot address, further supporting the waiver of such conservative treatment requirements.

For these reasons, the requested ACDF C4-C5 is medically necessary and should be approved.
