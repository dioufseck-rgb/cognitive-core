HealthFirst has completed the first-level internal appeal review of the prior authorization request for an anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) at the C5-C6 level. After a comprehensive review of your clinical records, the HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria, California regulatory requirements, and generally accepted standards of medical practice, our determination is as follows:

DISPOSITION: OVERTURN

The initial denial of coverage for the requested procedure is reversed. The procedure is now authorized.

CLINICAL SUMMARY
You are a 41-year-old professional violinist who presented with acute C5-C6 radiculopathy. Clinical examination on October 1, 2024, documented right C6 dermatomal numbness, a diminished bicep reflex, and 4/5 grip strength. Fine motor testing confirmed an inability to perform violin fingering tasks, which was further validated by occupational therapy as a significant functional impairment. An MRI dated October 1, 2024, demonstrated a large C5-C6 disc herniation with moderate right C6 nerve root compression and foraminal stenosis.

PLAN CRITERIA ANALYSIS
Per HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria CC-SPINE-2024, Section 2, authorization for ACDF is indicated when specific diagnostic and conservative treatment requirements are met:

1. Diagnosis (Section 2.1): You have a confirmed diagnosis of cervical radiculopathy with objective neurological deficits (4/5 grip strength) and correlating MRI findings. This criterion is satisfied.
2. Conservative Treatment (Section 2.2): The plan requires a minimum of 6 weeks of physical therapy, two pharmacotherapy agents for 4 weeks each, and one interventional treatment. Your records confirm:
   - Physical Therapy: 6 weeks of intensive therapy (18 sessions) completed with a documented functional plateau.
   - Pharmacotherapy: Trials of oral corticosteroids (6 weeks) and Gabapentin (5 weeks).
   - Interventional: A right C6 epidural steroid injection performed on October 28, 2024.

The initial denial stated the request was "premature" because symptoms had been present for "only 6 weeks." However, Section 2.2(a) requires a "minimum 6 weeks." As you have completed exactly 6 weeks of intensive therapy and reached a functional plateau, you have satisfied the plan's duration requirements.

REGULATORY COMPLIANCE
Under California Insurance Code (CIC) § 10169.5, a health plan shall not condition approval of a surgical procedure on the completion of physical therapy if the member has completed a course of therapy and the treating physician documents a "functional plateau." Your records explicitly document a functional plateau at 6 weeks with no improvement in fine motor deficits. Furthermore, California Health & Safety Code (CHSC) § 1374.32 requires that medical necessity be determined based on generally accepted standards of medical practice, regardless of whether the plan's internal criteria are more restrictive.

CLINICAL STANDARD OF REVIEW
Generally accepted clinical standards, including the AANS/CNS Joint Section Guidelines (2023) and NASS Evidence-Based Clinical Guidelines (2020), support surgical intervention for cervical radiculopathy when there is significant functional impairment that has not responded to conservative care. Your status as a professional musician with career-threatening motor deficits meets the Tier 2 "Reasonable Indication" for surgery under these guidelines.

RESOLUTION OF CONFLICTS
A conflict exists between the initial reviewer's interpretation of the 6-week requirement and the clinical reality of your functional plateau. Following the hierarchy of authority required by California law, the statutory protections regarding functional plateaus (CIC § 10169.5) and the clinical standards for functional impairment (AANS/CNS 2023) supersede the plan's mechanical application of a duration requirement. 

WARRANT FOR OVERTURN
The denial is overturned because you have satisfied all explicit requirements of the HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria. Furthermore, the documented functional plateau and the severity of your occupational impairment render further delay inconsistent with both California law and generally accepted standards of medical practice.
