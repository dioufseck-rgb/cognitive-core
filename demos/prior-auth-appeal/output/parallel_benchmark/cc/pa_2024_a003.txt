HealthFirst has completed the clinical appeal review for the requested cervical spinal surgery. After reviewing the clinical records, plan coverage criteria, California regulatory requirements, and generally accepted standards of medical practice, we have determined that the prior authorization denial issued on October 15, 2024, is OVERTURNED. The requested procedure is medically necessary and is now authorized.

CLINICAL SUMMARY
You are a 55-year-old female with a diagnosis of progressive cervical myelopathy at C4-C5. Clinical records dated August 25, 2024, document objective neurological deficits, including bilateral lower extremity spasticity, hyperreflexia, impaired fine motor skills in both hands, and a positive Lhermitte sign. Imaging (MRI dated August 20, 2024) confirms severe central cord compression at C4-C5 with T2 signal change (myelomalacia), indicating progressive spinal cord injury. You completed 5 weeks of physical therapy (15 sessions), which was discontinued due to your permanent pacemaker and a clinical determination that therapy was not arresting your neurological decline. An anesthesiology consultation (September 10, 2024) documented that cervical epidural steroid injections are medically contraindicated due to your pacemaker.

PLAN CRITERIA ANALYSIS
The initial denial was based on Section 2.2 of the HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria (CC-SPINE-2024), citing a failure to complete 6 weeks of physical therapy and a lack of interventional treatment. However, this review finds that the initial determination failed to apply the "EXCEPTION 2.2(A) — MYELOPATHY PATHWAY."

Under Section 2.2(A), for members with a primary diagnosis of cervical myelopathy:
1. The physical therapy requirement is reduced to 4 weeks. You completed 5 weeks, satisfying this requirement.
2. The interventional treatment requirement is waived if the physician documents a risk of neurological deterioration or medical contraindication. Your records include a formal contraindication from Dr. Rivera due to your permanent pacemaker.

Consequently, you met the Plan's internal medical necessity criteria at the time of the initial request.

REGULATORY COMPLIANCE
Furthermore, the denial is inconsistent with California law and regulatory guidance:
1. CIC § 10169.5(a)(3): Prohibits conditioning surgical approval on physical therapy when there is objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that therapy cannot address. Your documented myelomalacia constitutes such compromise.
2. DMHC All-Plan Letter (APL) 22-014: Specifically identifies myelomalacia on MRI as a finding that supports the waiver of conservative treatment requirements due to the risk of irreversible harm.
3. CHSC § 1374.32(b): Requires that medical necessity be determined by generally accepted standards of medical practice, which supersede more restrictive internal plan criteria.

CLINICAL STANDARD ANALYSIS
According to the AANS/CNS Joint Section Guidelines (2023) and NASS Evidence-Based Guidelines (2020), moderate-to-severe cervical myelopathy with myelomalacia is classified as a "Tier 1 — Strong Indication" for surgical decompression. These standards establish that delaying surgery to complete a physical therapy trial in the presence of cord signal change (myelomalacia) is not supported by clinical evidence and increases the risk of permanent neurological deficit.

RESOLUTION OF CONFLICTS
In this case, a conflict exists between the initial reviewer's mechanical application of the 6-week physical therapy rule and the higher-order requirements of California law and clinical standards. Following the required hierarchy of authority, California Insurance Code and the clinical standard for myelopathy management take precedence. The initial denial's failure to recognize the documented medical contraindication and the regulatory protections for myelopathy patients rendered the denial procedurally and clinically deficient.

WARRANT
The denial is overturned because you satisfied the Plan's own Myelopathy Pathway exceptions, and the requirements cited in the denial are unenforceable under CIC § 10169.5 and APL 22-014 given the objective evidence of progressive spinal cord injury (myelomalacia).
