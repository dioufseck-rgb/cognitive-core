Dear Ms. Morrison,

HealthFirst has completed the formal appeal review of the prior authorization request for a C3-C4 Anterior Cervical Discectomy and Fusion (ACDF). After a comprehensive evaluation of the clinical record, the HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria (CC-SPINE-2024), and the California Health and Safety Code, we have determined that the initial denial was in error. The requested procedure is MEDICALLY NECESSARY and is hereby APPROVED.

CLINICAL SUMMARY
You are a 67-year-old member with a history of a successful C4-C5 ACDF in 2019. You presented with new-onset left C4 radicular symptoms, including shoulder numbness and objective deltoid weakness (3+/5) on examination (08/20/2024). You have exhausted conservative management, including 10 weeks of physical therapy, six months of neuropathic pharmacotherapy (Pregabalin and Duloxetine), and a C4 epidural steroid injection which provided only transient relief.

PLAN CRITERIA ANALYSIS (CC-SPINE-2024)
The initial denial dated 10/01/2024 stated that imaging did not show sufficient pathology. However, our review finds that you satisfy all requirements under HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria CC-SPINE-2024:

1. DIAGNOSIS AND IMAGING (Section 2.1): The plan criteria explicitly state that a diagnosis may be confirmed via "MRI or CT myelogram." While your MRI (08/15/2024) was limited by "SIGNIFICANT ARTIFACT" from your 2019 hardware, the CT myelogram (09/10/2024) confirmed "moderate-severe left C4 nerve root compression" and "significant foraminal stenosis." The initial reviewer’s reliance on the artifact-limited MRI while ignoring the definitive CT myelogram findings constitutes a failure to apply Section 2.1 correctly.
2. CONSERVATIVE TREATMENT (Section 2.2): You completed 10 weeks of physical therapy (exceeding the 6-week requirement in 2.2(a)) and trialed two neuropathic agents (exceeding the 4-week requirement in 2.2(b)).
3. IMAGING CONSISTENCY (Section 2.3): The CT myelogram demonstrates structural pathology that is directly consistent with your clinical symptoms and the proposed surgical level.

CALIFORNIA REGULATORY STANDARD (CHSC § 1374.32)
Under California Health and Safety Code § 1374.32, a service is medically necessary if it is in accordance with generally accepted standards of medical practice and is clinically appropriate. The initial denial was procedurally defective under CHSC § 1374.31(b) because it failed to account for the most clinically accurate imaging data available in the record. Prudent clinical judgment dictates that when a primary imaging modality (MRI) is obscured by hardware artifact, a secondary definitive study (CT myelogram) must govern the clinical decision-making process.

CLINICAL EVIDENCE AND GUIDELINES
According to the AANS/CNS Joint Section on Disorders of the Spine (2023 Update), surgical intervention is indicated for radiculopathy with objective neurological deficits (Tier 2b) when conservative treatment has failed. Furthermore, generally accepted neurosurgical standards dictate that CT myelography is the gold standard for evaluating adjacent segment disease when MRI quality is degraded by metallic artifact. Relying on an imaging study acknowledged by the radiologist as unreliable due to artifact, while a definitive study is available, is inconsistent with these standards.

CONFLICT RESOLUTION AND WARRANT
A conflict existed between the initial reviewer's interpretation of the MRI and the surgeon's interpretation of the CT myelogram. Following the regulatory hierarchy, California law (CHSC § 1374.32) and generally accepted clinical standards (AANS/CNS 2023) require the use of the most reliable clinical evidence. Because the CT myelogram confirms pathology that meets both the plan’s internal criteria (Section 2.1) and the state’s medical necessity standard, the denial must be overturned.
