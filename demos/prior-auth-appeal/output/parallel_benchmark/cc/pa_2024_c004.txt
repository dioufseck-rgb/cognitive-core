Dear Mr. Flores,

HealthFirst has completed the clinical appeal review for the requested cervical spinal surgery (ACDF) at the C5-C6 level. After a thorough evaluation of your clinical records, the HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria, California regulatory requirements, and generally accepted medical standards, our decision is to UPHOLD the initial denial of coverage.

CLINICAL SUMMARY
Your clinical records, including an MRI dated September 15, 2024, demonstrate severe central canal stenosis at the C5-C6 level with a canal diameter of 6mm (normal is >13mm). The imaging shows spinal cord contact but specifically notes there is no cord compression, no T2 signal change, and no myelomalacia (spinal cord bruising). A neurological examination performed on September 20, 2024, was documented as "completely normal," with no signs of radiculopathy (nerve root pain/weakness) or myelopathy (spinal cord dysfunction). Your primary symptom is documented as axial neck pain (rated 5/10), which has shown modest improvement following a course of physical therapy, medications (Naproxen, Cyclobenzaprine), and a cervical epidural steroid injection.

PLAN CRITERIA DETERMINATION
Under HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria CC-SPINE-2024, Section 2.1 (Diagnosis Requirement), authorization for ACDF requires a confirmed diagnosis of cervical radiculopathy, cervical myelopathy, or a disc herniation with an objective neurological deficit. Your records do not document any of these conditions; rather, they confirm a normal neurological exam. Furthermore, Section 3.1 (Exclusions) explicitly states that surgery will be denied when requested solely for axial neck pain without documented radiculopathy or myelopathy. Because your symptoms are limited to axial neck pain and your neurological exam is normal, the request does not meet the plan's medical necessity criteria.

REGULATORY COMPLIANCE
We evaluated this request under California Health & Safety Code (CHSC) § 1374.32, which defines medical necessity as services provided in accordance with generally accepted standards of medical practice. While your surgeon, Dr. Davis, argues for prophylactic surgery due to your occupation in construction, the current clinical evidence does not support surgery in the absence of neurological symptoms. Additionally, we reviewed the request under Department of Managed Health Care (DMHC) All-Plan Letter (APL) 22-014. This regulation requires plans to waive certain requirements for urgent neurological conditions; however, urgency is defined by objective evidence of progressive pathology or myelomalacia. As your MRI specifically noted the absence of myelomalacia and your exam is stable/normal, the urgency exceptions do not apply.

CLINICAL EVIDENCE STANDARD
Generally accepted clinical standards, including the AANS/CNS Joint Section Guidelines (2023) and the North American Spine Society (NASS) Evidence-Based Clinical Guidelines (2020), categorize surgical intervention for axial neck pain without radiculopathy or myelopathy as "Tier 3: Surgery Not Indicated." Clinical evidence suggests that for patients with asymptomatic stenosis (even when severe), the risks of surgery outweigh the benefits until such time as neurological deficits or myelopathic symptoms develop. Prophylactic decompression for critically narrow canals in the absence of symptoms is not currently supported as a standard of care in the peer-reviewed literature cited in these guidelines.

HIERARCHY OF DETERMINATION
In this case, there is no conflict between the hierarchical sources. The HealthFirst internal criteria (CC-SPINE-2024), California state law (CHSC § 1374.32), and the national clinical standards (AANS/CNS 2023) all consistently indicate that surgical intervention is not medically necessary for your current clinical presentation.

WARRANT FOR DISPOSITION
The denial is UPHELD because the requested ACDF is for the treatment of axial neck pain in the setting of a normal neurological examination. While your imaging shows severe stenosis, the absence of radiculopathy, myelopathy, or myelomalacia means the procedure does not meet the definition of medical necessity under your plan's coverage documents or generally accepted medical practice.

INDEPENDENT MEDICAL REVIEW (IMR) RIGHTS
You have the right to request an Independent Medical Review (IMR) through the California Department of Managed Health Care (DMHC) if you believe this decision is incorrect. The IMR process is a review by an independent board of specialists not affiliated with HealthFirst. You may apply for an IMR at no cost to you. An IMR application form is enclosed with this letter, or you may apply online at www.dmhc.ca.gov.

NEXT STEPS
We recommend continued monitoring of your condition with your treating physician. Should you develop new symptoms such as weakness, numbness in your arms or legs, or changes in your gait or balance, please seek immediate medical evaluation, as these changes may alter the medical necessity of surgical intervention.
