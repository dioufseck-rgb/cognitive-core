DISPOSITION: REMAND FOR INDEPENDENT MEDICAL REVIEW (IMR)

After a comprehensive review of your clinical records, the plan’s internal criteria, and California regulatory requirements, we have determined that a final binary decision (to either uphold or overturn the denial) cannot be reached at this level due to an irreconcilable conflict in the radiological evidence. Per California Health & Safety Code (CHSC) § 1374.30, this case is being remanded for Independent Medical Review (IMR) to resolve the clinical dispute between qualified specialists.

CLINICAL SUMMARY
You are a 49-year-old female presenting with symptoms consistent with cervical myelopathy. Your neurological examination on October 5, 2024, documented objective Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) signs, including bilateral Hoffman signs and bilateral hyperreflexia in the upper extremities, along with subtle balance impairment. 

The core of the dispute involves the interpretation of your MRI dated October 1, 2024:
1. Radiologist A (Original): Interpreted findings as "equivocal" for T2 signal change, noting it may represent artifact.
2. Radiologist B (Treating Hospital): Confirmed early myelomalacia (cord damage), stating the T2 hyperintensity is reproducible across multiple sequences and is not an artifact.
3. Plan Radiologist (Dr. Foster): Interpreted the findings as artifact or a normal variant below the threshold for clinical significance.

PLAN CRITERIA APPLICATION
Under HealthFirst Clinical Coverage Criteria CC-SPINE-2024, Section 2.1(b), a diagnosis of cervical myelopathy requires spinal cord compression with myelopathic symptoms. Section 2.2(A) (Myelopathy Pathway) allows for the waiver of interventional treatments (like injections) if the myelopathy is "confirmed."

The plan’s initial denial was based on the determination that the myelopathy was not "confirmed" because the plan's reviewer classified the imaging findings as artifact. However, because the treating hospital’s radiologist has explicitly confirmed myelomalacia, the threshold for "confirmed cervical myelopathy" under Section 2.2(A) is contested. 

REGULATORY AND CLINICAL STANDARD ALIGNMENT
California Health & Safety Code § 1374.32 requires that medical necessity determinations be made in accordance with generally accepted standards of medical practice. Clinical guidelines from the AANS/CNS (2023) and NASS (2020) state that the presence of myelomalacia (cord signal change) is a primary surgical indication and often renders extended conservative trials, such as interventional injections, clinically inappropriate or contraindicated.

Under CHSC § 1374.30, when there is a direct conflict between the plan’s reviewer and the treating facility’s specialists regarding a critical diagnostic finding (the presence of myelomalacia), the plan cannot rely solely on its own internal interpretation to maintain a denial. Such a conflict creates a "medical necessity dispute" that requires resolution by a neutral third-party reviewer.

WARRANT FOR REMAND
A binary decision at this stage is inappropriate. To UPHOLD the denial would ignore the treating hospital's radiological confirmation of cord injury; to OVERTURN the denial would ignore the plan's internal radiological assessment of artifact. Because the "Myelopathy Exception" (Section 2.2A) hinges on whether the diagnosis is "confirmed," and that confirmation is currently the subject of a three-way radiological split, a REMAND for IMR is the only disposition that satisfies California regulatory standards for resolving contested clinical findings.

NEXT STEPS
Your case has been forwarded to the California Department of Managed Health Care (DMHC) for Independent Medical Review. The IMR organization will assign an independent board-certified neuroradiologist and neurosurgeon to provide a final, binding determination on the imaging and the necessity of the surgery. You will receive notification from the DMHC regarding the timeline for this final decision.
