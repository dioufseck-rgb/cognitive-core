**I. INTRODUCTION**

This letter serves as HealthFirst Plan's formal determination regarding the appeal for prior authorization of an Anterior Cervical Discectomy and Fusion (ACDF) C5-C6 for Ms. Priya Nair. The initial request was denied on October 25, 2024, citing a failure to meet the 6-week physical therapy requirement under HealthFirst's clinical coverage criteria CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2.

A comprehensive review of the appeal has been conducted, including the member's full clinical record, HealthFirst's clinical coverage criteria, relevant California state laws and regulations, and generally accepted standards of medical practice from authoritative clinical guidelines.

---

**II. BACKGROUND OF DENIAL AND APPEAL BASIS**

The initial denial was based on the determination that Ms. Nair had not completed the 6-week minimum physical therapy (PT) requirement specified in Section 2.2 of HealthFirst's CC-SPINE-2024 policy. Ms. Nair's treating physician appealed this decision, asserting that:
1.  HealthFirst incorrectly applied the standard pathway (Section 2.2) when the Myelopathy Exception (Section 2.2A) of the policy is applicable.
2.  Ms. Nair has a confirmed diagnosis of cervical myelopathy, evidenced by bilateral Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) signs and cord compression on MRI.
3.  The Myelopathy Exception requires a minimum of 4 weeks of PT, and Ms. Nair completed 5 weeks, thereby exceeding the applicable requirement.
4.  Under the correct pathway, all criteria for surgical authorization are met.

---

**III. EVALUATION PLAN AND FINDINGS**

The appeal was evaluated according to a structured plan, applying the hierarchy of authority for conflict resolution: **State law > Clinical standard > Plan criteria.**

**1. Procedural Adequacy of Denial Notice (CHSC § 1374.31(b))**
*   **Purpose:** To determine if the initial denial notice was legally sufficient.
*   **Finding:** The initial denial notice, dated 2024-10-25, clearly specified the clinical criteria applied (CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2) and the specific clinical reason for the denial. It is presumed to have included notice of IMR rights. Therefore, the denial notice is deemed procedurally adequate on its face.
*   **Citation:** California Health & Safety Code (CHSC) § 1374.31(b).

**2. Diagnosis Confirmation and Classification**
*   **Purpose:** To accurately classify the member's primary diagnosis.
*   **Finding:** Review of the medical record confirms a primary diagnosis of cervical myelopathy at C5-C6. This is supported by objective evidence including:
    *   MRI (2024-09-01) demonstrating moderate central cord compression at C5-C6.
    *   Neurological exam (2024-09-05) revealing bilateral Hoffman signs, bilateral hyperreflexia upper extremities, mildly impaired tandem gait, and bilateral hand clumsiness, all confirming upper motor neuron signs.
    *   Neurologist consultation (2024-09-12) confirming the clinical picture is "consistent with cervical myelopathy. Bilateral UMN signs are pathognomonic. Diagnosis: cervical myelopathy, primary." (ICD-10: G35.89).
*   This aligns with HealthFirst's CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.1(b) for cervical myelopathy. It is noted that the MRI report specifically states "No T2 signal change. No myelomalacia."
*   **Citation:** Clinical record (presenting_diagnosis, imaging, neurological_exam, diagnosis_confirmation), HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.1(b).

**3. Plan Criteria Application Review (HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024)**
*   **Purpose:** To assess whether the initial reviewer correctly applied HealthFirst's own clinical coverage criteria.
*   **Finding:** Based on the confirmed primary diagnosis of cervical myelopathy (from Step 2), HealthFirst's own policy, CC-SPINE-2024, Section 2.2, EXCEPTION 2.2(A) — MYELOPATHY PATHWAY, is the applicable criterion.
    *   This exception states: "For members with confirmed cervical myelopathy (criterion 2.1(b)), the conservative treatment requirement is MODIFIED as follows: Physical therapy requirement is reduced to 4 weeks minimum. Interventional treatment requirement (2.2(c)) is WAIVED if treating physician documents that interventional treatment poses risk of neurological deterioration."
    *   The member completed 5 weeks of physical therapy, which exceeds the 4-week minimum required under the Myelopathy Exception.
    *   The interventional treatment requirement was appropriately waived by the treating physician, Dr. Chen, who documented: "Interventional procedure not indicated — cord compression with myelopathy requires surgical evaluation, not injection."
    *   The pharmacotherapy requirement was also met with a trial of Baclofen for 3 months and Methylprednisolone for 3 weeks.
*   Therefore, the initial denial was a misapplication of HealthFirst's own criteria, as the Myelopathy Exception (2.2A) was not applied, and under the correct exception, all requirements are satisfied.
*   **Citation:** HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2 and Exception 2.2(A); clinical record (conservative_treatment).

**4. State Law Compliance Review (CIC § 10169.5 & DMHC APL 22-014)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine if California state law provisions override or modify the plan's conservative treatment requirements.
*   **Finding:** The member's condition, with confirmed cervical myelopathy, objective cord compression, and UMN signs, triggers protections under California state law:
    *   **California Insurance Code (CIC) § 10169.5(a)(3):** This provision prohibits a plan from conditioning approval of a surgical procedure on completion of physical therapy where "The condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address." Ms. Nair's diagnosis of cervical myelopathy with confirmed cord compression and UMN signs constitutes objective structural neurological compromise for which physical therapy is not expected to address the underlying neurological deficit, as confirmed by the treating physician halting PT due to lack of improvement in UMN signs.
    *   **DMHC All-Plan Letter (APL) 22-014 (II.3):** This guidance states that "Plans shall not apply internal criteria that are more restrictive than generally accepted standards of medical practice for conditions identified in this guidance." Cervical myelopathy is a time-sensitive neurological condition explicitly identified within this APL. While the absence of myelomalacia means that specific urgency triggers related to myelomalacia in APL 22-014 (II.1 and II.2(a)) are not met, the general principle of not applying overly restrictive criteria to myelopathy remains applicable.
*   **Citation:** California Insurance Code (CIC) § 10169.5(a)(3); DMHC APL 22-014 II.3; clinical record (imaging, neurological_exam, physical_therapy_summary).

**5. Clinical Standard Compliance Review (AANS/CNS 2023, AO Spine 2021, NASS 2020)**
*   **Purpose:** To determine if generally accepted clinical standards of medical practice support surgical intervention regardless of plan criteria.
*   **Finding:** The member's condition, moderate cervical myelopathy with objective cord compression and definitive upper motor neuron signs (bilateral Hoffman, hyperreflexia, gait impairment, hand clumsiness), meets TIER 1 ("Strong Indication") for surgical intervention per the AANS/CNS Joint Guidelines for Management of Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy (2023 Update).
    *   Leading clinical guidelines, including AO Spine Guidelines (2021) and the Cochrane Review (2021), consistently state that there is "no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy." They further indicate that applying radiculopathy-derived PT requirements to myelopathy patients is a "category error" not supported by evidence and that delaying surgery in such patients may result in neurological deterioration.
    *   Therefore, generally accepted clinical standards of medical practice mandate approval for surgery for this clinical presentation and deem any requirement for further conservative physical therapy inappropriate.
*   **Citation:** AANS/CNS Joint Section on Disorders of the Spine: Guidelines for Management of Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy (2023 Update) Section 3.1 TIER 1(a); AO Spine International: Clinical Practice Guideline (2021) Section 2.1; Cochrane Review: Surgery vs Conservative Management for Cervical Myelopathy, Nikolaidis et al. (2021) Section 2.1; Clinical Reference Summary Sections 1.1, 2.1, 2.2, 3.1, 3.2.

**IV. CONFLICT RESOLUTION AND FINAL DISPOSITION**

The evaluation reveals that the initial denial of prior authorization for Ms. Nair's requested ACDF C5-C6 was in error on multiple grounds:

1.  **Misapplication of Plan Criteria:** HealthFirst's own clinical coverage criteria (CC-SPINE-2024) includes a Myelopathy Exception (Section 2.2A) that applies to Ms. Nair's confirmed primary diagnosis of cervical myelopathy. Under this exception, the physical therapy requirement is 4 weeks, which the member met and exceeded (5 weeks completed).
2.  **Violation of State Law (CIC § 10169.5):** California law prohibits conditioning surgical approval on physical therapy where there is "objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address," which applies directly to Ms. Nair's cervical myelopathy with cord compression and UMN signs.
3.  **Inconsistency with Clinical Standards:** Generally accepted clinical standards of medical practice strongly support surgical intervention for moderate cervical myelopathy with objective cord compression and UMN signs. These standards explicitly caution against delaying surgery for mandatory physical therapy trials in such cases, citing a lack of evidence and potential for neurological deterioration. DMHC APL 22-014 further reinforces that plans should not apply internal criteria more restrictive than generally accepted standards for myelopathy.

Applying the hierarchy of authority (State law > Clinical standard > Plan criteria), the requirements of both California state law and generally accepted clinical standards override any more restrictive interpretations of HealthFirst's internal criteria. Furthermore, even under a correct application of HealthFirst's own criteria, the service meets medical necessity.

**Therefore, the initial denial is OVERTURNED.**
