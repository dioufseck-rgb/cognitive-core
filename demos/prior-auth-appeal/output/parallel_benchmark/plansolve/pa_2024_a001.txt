This letter serves as the formal determination regarding your appeal for the previously denied prior authorization request for an anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) at C5-C6. HealthFirst Health Plan has completed a comprehensive review of your appeal, medical records, our internal clinical criteria, and applicable state and federal laws and generally accepted standards of medical practice.

**1. Requested Service & Initial Denial:**
On November 1, 2024, HealthFirst Health Plan denied the prior authorization request for CPT code 22551 (ACDF C5-C6, one level). The denial was based on reason code MED-003, stating: "Member has completed only 2 weeks of physical therapy. Standard pathway requires minimum 6 weeks. Pharmacotherapy requirement not met: only one agent trialed. Member does not meet conservative treatment requirements under CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2."

**2. Appeal Basis:**
Your appeal, submitted by Dr. James Kim on your behalf, asserted the following:
*   You have severe myelomalacia on MRI, and California law prohibits a physical therapy (PT) requirement when structural neurological compromise is present.
*   Your treating physician declares PT contraindicated due to documented cord signal change.
*   Regulatory override under California Insurance Code (CIC) 10169.5(a)(3) makes plan criteria irrelevant in this specific clinical scenario.

**3. Review Process:**
This appeal was reviewed by an expert health plan appeals reviewer. The review followed a strict hierarchy of authority governing conflict resolution:
1.  **State Law** (California Insurance Code, Health & Safety Code, DMHC All-Plan Letters)
2.  **Clinical Standard** (AANS/CNS, NASS, AO Spine Guidelines)
3.  **Plan Criteria** (HealthFirst CC-SPINE-2024)

Where sources conflict, the higher authority governs.

**4. Evaluation Findings:**

**Step 1: Procedural Adequacy of Denial Notice (CHSC § 1374.31(b))**
The initial denial notice dated 2024-11-01 specified the clinical criteria applied (CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2) and provided specific clinical reasons for denial. Assuming IMR rights were included, the denial notice is deemed procedurally adequate.

**Step 2: Confirmation of Member's Primary Diagnosis and Clinical Presentation**
A thorough review of your clinical record confirms the following:
*   **Presenting Diagnosis:** Progressive cervical myelopathy C5-C6 with myelomalacia.
*   **Clinical Presentation:** Four-month history of bilateral hand clumsiness and gait instability. Neurological exam reveals objective upper motor neuron signs including bilateral Hoffman signs, hyperreflexia in bilateral upper and lower extremities, and positive Babinski bilateral, along with documented gait ataxia.
*   **Imaging:** MRI dated 2024-08-15 demonstrates severe central cord compression at C5-C6 with T2 hyperintensity within the spinal cord (myelomalacia).
These findings conclusively establish a diagnosis of moderate-to-severe cervical myelopathy with objective evidence of spinal cord compression, upper motor neuron signs, and myelomalacia.

**Step 3: HealthFirst Plan Criteria Application (CC-SPINE-2024)**
The initial denial stated that the "Standard pathway requires minimum 6 weeks" of physical therapy and cited HealthFirst's CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2. However, HealthFirst's own criteria, under **EXCEPTION 2.2(A) — MYELOPATHY PATHWAY**, explicitly states that for members with confirmed cervical myelopathy, the physical therapy requirement is reduced to a minimum of 4 weeks, and the interventional treatment requirement may be waived. The plan failed to apply its own myelopathy exception, incorrectly applying the standard 6-week PT requirement for a primary myelopathy diagnosis. The pharmacotherapy requirement (trial of at least two agents) was noted as unmet, which remains a requirement even under the myelopathy exception.

**Step 4: California Regulatory Overrides (CIC § 10169.5 & DMHC APL 22-014)**
California state laws and regulations are determined to override the plan's conservative treatment requirements in your case:
*   **California Insurance Code (CIC) § 10169.5(a)(3):** This statute explicitly states that a health insurer shall not condition approval of a surgical procedure on completion of physical therapy where "the condition involves objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address." Your MRI findings of "T2 hyperintensity within cord (myelomalacia)" constitute objective evidence of structural neurological compromise. Furthermore, your treating physician, Dr. James Kim, specifically documented that PT is contraindicated due to myelomalacia and the risk of irreversible cord injury, citing CIC 10169.5(a)(3). This provision directly negates any mandatory physical therapy requirement in your case.
*   **DMHC All-Plan Letter (APL) 22-014:** This guidance for Time-Sensitive Neurological Conditions states that plans SHALL NOT require completion of a full course of conservative treatment when objective imaging demonstrates progressive pathology (such as cord signal change or myelomalacia), the treating physician documents conservative failure, and surgical intervention is indicated under generally accepted guidelines. The presence of myelomalacia on your MRI is specifically identified in this APL as a finding that supports the waiver of standard conservative treatment requirements. Your physician's documentation of worsening symptoms during PT and the declaration of contraindication, combined with the myelomalacia, meets these criteria, overriding both the PT and broader conservative treatment requirements.

**Step 5: Clinical Standard Override (CHSC § 1374.32(b) and Clinical Guidelines)**
The requested service also meets generally accepted standards of medical practice, which override more restrictive plan criteria (CHSC § 1374.32(b)):
*   **AANS/CNS Joint Section Guidelines (2023 Update):** Your diagnosis of moderate-to-severe cervical myelopathy with objective cord compression and myelomalacia, accompanied by upper motor neuron signs, is classified as a TIER 1 strong indication for surgical intervention.
*   **AO Spine International Clinical Practice Guideline (2021):** These guidelines unequivocally state that "There is no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy. Delaying surgery in such patients to complete a physical therapy trial is not supported by the evidence and may result in neurological deterioration." The presence of myelomalacia indicates existing cord injury and a higher risk of permanent deficit if surgery is delayed.
Based on these widely recognized clinical standards, delaying surgery to mandate further physical therapy or an extended trial of pharmacotherapy in your specific condition is inconsistent with generally accepted medical practice and carries a risk of irreversible neurological harm.

**5. Determination:**

Based on the comprehensive review and in accordance with the established hierarchy of authority, HealthFirst Health Plan's internal criteria regarding conservative treatment requirements are superseded by California state law and generally accepted clinical standards of medical practice.

**The appeal is OVERTURNED.**

**Rationale:**
The initial denial incorrectly applied the standard conservative treatment pathway without acknowledging or properly applying HealthFirst's own Myelopathy Exception (CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2A). More critically, California law (CIC § 10169.5(a)(3)) explicitly prohibits requiring physical therapy when objective evidence of structural neurological compromise, such as myelomalacia, is present, or when the treating physician declares it contraindicated. Furthermore, DMHC APL 22-014 mandates the waiver of full conservative treatment requirements in cases of progressive pathology with myelomalacia. Finally, generally accepted clinical guidelines from leading spine societies strongly advocate for surgical intervention in moderate-to-severe cervical myelopathy with myelomalacia and explicitly caution against delaying surgery for mandatory conservative treatment trials, as this poses a significant risk of irreversible neurological deterioration. Your clinical presentation meets the criteria for urgent surgical intervention under these higher authorities.
