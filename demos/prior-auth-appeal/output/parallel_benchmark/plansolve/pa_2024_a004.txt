**Dear Mr. Chang, Dr. Marchetti, and HealthFirst Health Plan,**

This letter serves as the formal determination of the appeal for prior authorization request PA-2024-A004 for Robert Chang, regarding a requested anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) at C5-C6. This determination is based on a comprehensive review of the submitted clinical record, HealthFirst's clinical coverage criteria (CC-SPINE-2024), relevant California state laws, and generally accepted clinical standards of medical practice.

**1. Background of Request and Denial:**

*   **Initial Request:** Prior authorization was requested by Dr. Elena Marchetti, MD, Neurosurgery, for CPT 22551 (ACDF C5-C6) for Mr. Robert Chang, noting an "EMERGENCY" presentation.
*   **Initial Denial:** On 2024-11-18, HealthFirst issued a denial (Reason Code: MED-003) stating, "Physical therapy not completed: 0 weeks documented. No pharmacotherapy documented. No interventional procedure documented. Member has not initiated conservative treatment. Request denied pending completion of required conservative treatment under CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2." The denial was issued by Dr. Patricia Huang, MD, at the initial review level.
*   **Appeal Basis:** The appeal highlighted:
    *   Acute myelopathy presentation where physical therapy (PT) was contraindicated and would cause immediate harm.
    *   MRI showing severe cord compression with extensive myelomalacia on initial presentation, indicating an emergency.
    *   Applicability of California Insurance Code (CIC) 10169.5(a)(3) from day one.

**2. Clinical Summary:**

Mr. Robert Chang, a 38-year-old male, presented to the ER on 2024-11-10 with acute onset bilateral arm weakness and difficulty walking over 72 hours, with no prior cervical history.

*   **Presenting Diagnosis:** Acute cervical myelopathy with rapidly progressive deficits—surgical emergency.
*   **Neurological Exam (2024-11-10):** Bilateral arm weakness 2/5, bilateral leg weakness 3/5, hyperreflexia all extremities, bilateral Babinski, urinary urgency—findings consistent with an incomplete spinal cord syndrome.
*   **Imaging (MRI 2024-11-10):** "URGENT: Severe C5-C6 central cord compression. Extensive T2 hyperintensity throughout cord (extensive myelomalacia). Findings consistent with acute cord injury. Neurosurgery emergently consulted."
*   **Conservative Treatment Attempted:**
    *   **Physical Therapy:** 0 weeks documented. The treating physician (Dr. Marchetti) explicitly declared PT "absolutely contraindicated on initial presentation" due to extensive myelomalacia on emergency MRI, stating that "Initiating PT would risk converting incomplete cord syndrome to complete cord syndrome."
    *   **Pharmacotherapy:** IV methylprednisolone administered for 24 hours inpatient as per standard acute spinal cord injury protocol in the ER.
    *   **Interventional:** None.
*   **Physician Declaration:** Dr. Marchetti stated, "This patient has an acute incomplete cervical cord syndrome with extensive myelomalacia. PT is absolutely contraindicated and would be dangerous. CIC 10169.5(a)(3) exception applies. Delaying surgery risks permanent complete paralysis. Emergency authorization requested."

**3. Evaluation Plan and Findings:**

This review followed the specified Evaluation Plan, prioritizing State Law, then Clinical Standards, and finally Plan Criteria in case of conflict.

**Step 1: Procedural Adequacy of Denial Notice**
*   **Finding:** Positive. The initial denial notice dated 2024-11-18 specified the clinical criteria applied ("CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2") and a specific clinical reason for denial. This meets the requirements of California Health & Safety Code (CHSC) § 1374.31(b).

**Step 2: Diagnosis Classification and Primary Condition Determination**
*   **Finding:** Positive. The member has a confirmed primary diagnosis of acute cervical myelopathy with rapidly progressive deficits. This is supported by objective findings including severe spinal cord compression and extensive myelomalacia on MRI, bilateral upper and lower extremity weakness, hyperreflexia, bilateral Babinski signs, and urinary urgency, all indicative of upper motor neuron involvement and spinal cord dysfunction. This aligns with HealthFirst's CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.1(b) criteria for cervical myelopathy and accepted medical domain knowledge.

**Step 3: Presence of Myelomalacia or Objective Structural Neurological Compromise**
*   **Finding:** Positive. The MRI performed on 2024-11-10 clearly demonstrates "Severe C5-C6 central cord compression" and "Extensive T2 hyperintensity throughout cord (extensive myelomalacia)." Myelomalacia signifies existing spinal cord injury/damage and represents objective structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address (Clinical Standard 1.2, 3.1(c)). The requested procedure's "EMERGENCY" status further emphasizes the urgency.

**Step 4: Application of Plan's Internal Myelopathy Exception**
*   **Finding:** Positive (Plan Misapplication). HealthFirst's own CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2(A) EXCEPTION states that for confirmed cervical myelopathy, the physical therapy requirement is reduced to a 4-week minimum, and the interventional treatment requirement is WAIVED if the treating physician documents risk of neurological deterioration. The initial denial cited standard Section 2.2 requirements (6 weeks PT, interventional) despite Dr. Marchetti's clear documentation that PT was "absolutely contraindicated" and would be "dangerous" due to extensive myelomalacia and the risk of converting an incomplete cord syndrome to a complete one. The initial reviewer failed to apply the plan's own myelopathy exception which would have, at minimum, waived the interventional requirement and critically considered the physician's contraindication for PT.

**Step 5: Assessment Against California State Law Overrides (CIC 10169.5 & DMHC APL 22-014)**
*   **Finding:** Positive. California state laws explicitly override the plan's conservative treatment requirements in this case:
    *   **CIC § 10169.5(a)(1):** The treating physician, Dr. Marchetti, documented in writing that physical therapy is "absolutely contraindicated" and "dangerous" given the nature and severity of Mr. Chang's acute cervical myelopathy with myelomalacia. This provision prohibits the plan from conditioning approval of surgery on PT completion.
    *   **CIC § 10169.5(a)(3):** The MRI showing "Severe C5-C6 central cord compression" and "Extensive myelomalacia" constitutes "objective evidence of structural neurological compromise that physical therapy cannot address." This provision also prohibits the plan from requiring PT.
    *   **CIC § 10169.5(c):** The initial denial mechanically applied a "0 weeks documented" PT finding without considering the neurological urgency and the physician's documented contraindication, violating this section.
    *   **DMHC All-Plan Letter (APL) 22-014 (II.1 & II.2):** This APL mandates that cervical myelopathy with objective cord compression and myelomalacia be treated as urgent (II.1). It prohibits plans from requiring a full course of conservative treatment (including PT) when objective imaging (like myelomalacia) demonstrates progressive pathology and surgery is indicated under generally accepted guidelines (II.2). The presence of myelomalacia is specifically identified as a finding that supports waiver of standard conservative treatment. The initial denial directly conflicts with these regulatory requirements.
    *   **DMHC APL 22-014 (II.3):** The plan's criteria, as applied in the initial denial, are more restrictive than generally accepted standards for this urgent myelopathic condition, which is prohibited.

**Step 6: Assessment Against Generally Accepted Clinical Standards**
*   **Finding:** Positive. The requested surgery is medically necessary and indicated under generally accepted standards of medical practice:
    *   According to AANS/CNS Joint Section Guidelines (2023) and NASS Guidelines (2020), Mr. Chang's presentation of moderate-to-severe cervical myelopathy with objective cord compression, extensive myelomalacia, and rapidly progressive neurological deficits meets TIER 1 ("STRONG INDICATION") for surgical intervention (Clinical Standard 3.1(a, c, d)).
    *   Clinical guidelines, including the AO Spine Guidelines (2021) and Cochrane Review (2021), unequivocally state that there is **no high-quality evidence supporting mandatory physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with moderate to severe cervical myelopathy, particularly with myelomalacia.** Delaying surgery in such patients to complete a physical therapy trial is not supported by evidence and may result in neurological deterioration (Clinical Standard 1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.3).
    *   The application of conservative treatment requirements primarily derived from radiculopathy to a patient with acute myelopathy and myelomalacia is a "category error" not supported by evidence.
    *   CHSC § 1374.32(b) mandates that if a service meets generally accepted standards of medical practice, it SHALL be approved, regardless of whether the plan's internal criteria are more restrictive.

**4. Conflict Resolution and Final Determination:**

The hierarchy of authority for this review is State Law > Clinical Standard > Plan Criteria. Where sources conflict, the higher authority governs.

In this case, the initial denial was based on HealthFirst's CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2, requiring conservative treatment. However, the comprehensive review reveals that:

1.  **California State Law (CIC § 10169.5 and DMHC APL 22-014)** directly prohibits the plan from requiring conservative treatment (specifically physical therapy) due to the objective evidence of severe structural neurological compromise (myelomalacia and cord compression) and the treating physician's documented contraindication of PT. State law explicitly states that myelopathy with myelomalacia must be treated as urgent and cannot be held to full conservative treatment requirements.
2.  **Generally Accepted Clinical Standards** overwhelmingly support immediate surgical intervention for acute, moderate-to-severe cervical myelopathy with myelomalacia and progressive neurological deficits, without requiring a trial of physical therapy. These standards indicate that delaying surgery in such circumstances is not medically appropriate and may lead to irreversible harm.
3.  **HealthFirst's own internal Myelopathy Exception (CC-SPINE-2024 Section 2.2(A))** was not correctly applied by the initial reviewer, which would have modified the conservative treatment requirements.

Given the clear and compelling evidence from State Law and Clinical Standards, which supersede HealthFirst's plan criteria, the denial is contrary to established medical necessity principles and regulatory requirements.

**Disposition: OVERTURN**

The denial for prior authorization request PA-2024-A004 for Robert Chang for CPT 22551 (ACDF C5-C6) is hereby **OVERTURNED**.
