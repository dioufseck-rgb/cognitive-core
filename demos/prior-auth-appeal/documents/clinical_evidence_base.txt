SECTION 1 — DIAGNOSTIC CLASSIFICATION

1.1 CERVICAL MYELOPATHY vs. RADICULOPATHY — DISTINCTION

Cervical Radiculopathy:
- Compression of individual nerve root
- Unilateral or dermatomal symptoms
- Pain, sensory change, weakness in single nerve distribution
- Conservative treatment typically effective (70-80% resolution)

Cervical Myelopathy:
- Compression of spinal cord itself (not just nerve root)
- Bilateral symptoms common
- Upper motor neuron signs: hyperreflexia, Hoffman sign, clonus
- Gait disturbance, bilateral hand weakness, clumsiness
- Progressive course without treatment
- Conservative treatment has LIMITED efficacy for established myelopathy

CRITICAL DISTINCTION FOR TREATMENT DECISIONS:
Myelopathy and radiculopathy require fundamentally different treatment
algorithms. The evidence base for conservative treatment is primarily derived
from radiculopathy studies. Application of radiculopathy conservative
treatment requirements to myelopathy patients is NOT supported by clinical
evidence.

1.2 MYELOMALACIA — SIGNIFICANCE
Myelomalacia (T2 signal change within the spinal cord on MRI) indicates:
- Existing spinal cord injury/damage
- Higher risk of permanent deficit with continued compression
- Reduced likelihood of recovery even after successful decompression
- Surgical urgency — cord signal change is a predictor of poor outcome
  if surgery is delayed

Presence of myelomalacia on MRI is considered by major spine societies to
be a strong indication for surgical intervention and a contraindication to
extended conservative treatment trials.

SECTION 2 — EVIDENCE ON CONSERVATIVE TREATMENT FOR MYELOPATHY

2.1 PHYSICAL THERAPY FOR MYELOPATHY
Evidence level: LOW to MODERATE

Key findings from Cochrane Review (2021):
- Physical therapy produces modest short-term pain reduction in mild
  cervical myelopathy (modified Japanese Orthopaedic Association score > 12)
- For moderate-severe myelopathy (mJOA score 8-12): physical therapy
  does NOT produce meaningful functional improvement
- For severe myelopathy (mJOA < 8): conservative treatment is NOT
  recommended as primary treatment

From NASS Guidelines (2020), Grade B Evidence:
"Conservative treatment may be considered for mild cervical myelopathy
as initial management, with close monitoring for progression. For
moderate to severe myelopathy, surgical decompression is recommended
as the primary treatment."

Critical quote from AO Spine Guidelines (2021):
"There is no high-quality evidence supporting a mandatory course of
physical therapy prior to surgical intervention for patients with
moderate to severe cervical myelopathy. Delaying surgery in such
patients to complete a physical therapy trial is not supported by
the evidence and may result in neurological deterioration."

2.2 CONSERVATIVE TREATMENT DURATION — EVIDENCE SUMMARY
For myelopathy specifically:
- No randomized controlled trials support a defined minimum duration
  of conservative treatment before surgery
- The 6-week PT requirement in many payer policies derives from
  radiculopathy evidence, NOT myelopathy evidence
- Applying radiculopathy-derived PT requirements to myelopathy patients
  is a category error not supported by evidence

2.3 RISK OF DELAY
From multiple prospective studies:
- Each month of delay in surgical treatment for moderate-severe myelopathy
  is associated with decreased likelihood of neurological recovery
- Myelomalacia on preoperative MRI is the strongest predictor of
  incomplete neurological recovery postoperatively
- Patients who progress to severe myelopathy before surgery have
  significantly worse outcomes than those treated earlier

SECTION 3 — SURGICAL INDICATIONS: CLINICAL STANDARD

3.1 ACCEPTED INDICATIONS FOR ACDF (CPT 22551)
Per AANS/CNS Joint Guidelines (2023), surgery is indicated when:

TIER 1 — STRONG INDICATION (surgery recommended):
(a) Moderate to severe myelopathy with objective cord compression on MRI
(b) Progressive neurological deficit despite conservative treatment
(c) Myelomalacia on MRI (cord signal change)
(d) Acute severe myelopathy with risk of permanent deficit

TIER 2 — REASONABLE INDICATION (surgery reasonable option):
(a) Mild myelopathy with stable symptoms after adequate conservative trial
(b) Radiculopathy with persistent symptoms after 6-12 weeks conservative tx
(c) Significant functional impairment not responding to conservative care

TIER 3 — SURGERY NOT INDICATED:
(a) Axial neck pain without radiculopathy or myelopathy
(b) Imaging findings without correlating symptoms
(c) Mild radiculopathy not yet trialed on conservative treatment

3.2 APPLICATION TO MYELOPATHY CASES WITH MYELOMALACIA
Patients with:
- Confirmed cervical myelopathy (clinical and imaging)
- Myelomalacia on MRI
- Objective neurological deficits (Hoffman sign, hyperreflexia,
  bilateral weakness, gait disturbance)

Meet TIER 1 criteria. The clinical standard does NOT require completion
of a minimum course of physical therapy as a prerequisite to surgery for
this presentation. Requiring PT completion in this setting is inconsistent
with accepted standards of medical practice.

SECTION 5 — FUNCTIONAL PLATEAU DEFINITION (Clinical Standard)

Functional plateau in physical therapy is defined as:
- Less than 10% improvement in validated functional outcome measure
  (NDI, VAS, mJOA) over 3 consecutive therapy sessions, OR
- Therapist documentation that patient has reached maximum therapeutic
  benefit

Note: A patient who achieves partial pain reduction but no improvement
in neurological symptoms (weakness, reflex changes) after 6-8 weeks
of PT has reached functional plateau by clinical standard, even if
pain has not fully resolved. Neurological symptoms in myelopathy
(weakness, hyperreflexia) are not expected to respond to PT regardless
of duration.
